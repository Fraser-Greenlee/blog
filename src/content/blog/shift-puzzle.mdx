Years ago I made a little mobile puzzle that I always liked, so I rebuilt it for the web. It plays like a *2D Rubik's cube*.

You're shown two boards: a **target** on top, and **your board** below. Both are grids with a handful of coloured cells. Your board starts as a scrambled copy of the target, and the goal is to slide it back into alignment.

The controls sit around your board, one per edge and one per corner:

- The four **edge arrows** shift the whole board one step in that direction. Cells that fall off one side wrap around to the other.
- The four **corner buttons** shift the cells one step *around each ring*. The right-hand corners move them clockwise, the left-hand corners anticlockwise. On a larger grid the outer ring and the inner ring both rotate together; the very centre cell of an odd grid stays put.

Every move acts on the whole board at once, so the colours keep their shape — you're really just sliding a rigid pattern around a torus until it lands where you need it.

On the harder levels the goal is only a small **window** — a 3×3 region, outlined on your board — that has to match the target, while the rest of the board is free workspace. That's what smooths the difficulty out: a bigger board gives you more room to manoeuvre pieces into place, but the pattern you're aiming for stays the same modest size. The cells outside the window can end up any colour; only the window has to be right.

The fun part is the scoring. Every board is generated by computing its true shortest solution — a breadth-first search that, for whole-board goals, meets in the middle from both ends — and boards that turn out too easy are thrown away. From that proven optimum each board gets a **par**: a budget of moves above the shortest possible solution. Reach the target within par and you've solved it; the tighter you beat par, the better. Because the optimum is exact, par is meaningful: when it's 12, the shortest possible solution really is a handful of moves under that. The search runs in a Web Worker so the page never stutters while it's thinking.

There's no undo button. Instead, the move that *reverses* your last one steps you back and refunds it — and it's highlighted, so the way back out of a dead end is always one obvious tap. Exploring costs nothing as long as you retrace it.

A difficulty ladder introduces one idea at a time: it starts as a plain 3×3 with no move limit, then adds par, then more colours; then it brings in the window with a few spare "decoy" pieces cluttering the workspace and no limit, then adds the limit, then removes the decoys so every piece has to land, and finally adds more colours and cells. The next levels add *facing*: each piece carries a direction (a little arrow) that has to match the target as well as the colour, and — the fun part — a piece only turns as it leaves a corner of its ring, so a piece sliding along a straight edge keeps pointing the same way, while the centre piece spins in place. The next levels turn the cells into tiles of a *picture* to reassemble (a sliding-image puzzle on a torus), with turning and hidden-target variants where you don't even see the finished picture until you've solved it. Each step changes just one thing, so the jump is never steep, and there's a custom mode if you want to dial in the grid, colours, cells and difficulty yourself.

Finally there's a set of *candy* modes that flip the whole thing into an endless match game: line up a full row or column of matching candies to clear them, everything falls, and fresh candies drop in. You play against a move budget — every move costs one and every cleared line earns some back, so you have to keep matching or the run ends; the goal is the highest score before you run dry. Variants layer on facing-matching, bad candies you mustn't let line up, and inert blanks. The *shapes* variants are the most fun: instead of full lines, you're given a small coloured shape to form anywhere on the board (with a preview of the next one so you can set up combos), optionally needing the pieces to point a certain way, or even two different shapes you must assemble at the same time. The subtle part is that every target is generated from the colours actually on your board, so it's always achievable. It tracks how you do per level (solves and moves, or best score), all stored locally in your browser. It's built to feel good on a phone.
