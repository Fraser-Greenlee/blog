A little browser game that plays like a *2D Rubik's cube* crossed with a match-3.

You have a 3×3 board of coloured candies and shift the whole thing around to build the shape shown under **Make**. The controls sit around the board, one per edge and one per corner:

- The four **edge arrows** shift the whole board one step in that direction. Candies that fall off one side wrap around to the other.
- The four **corner buttons** shift the candies one step *around each ring* — the right-hand corners clockwise, the left-hand corners anticlockwise. The centre candy stays put.

Every move acts on the whole board at once, so you're sliding a rigid pattern around a torus until the candies you want line up into the target shape. Form it and those candies clear, everything falls, fresh candies drop in, and the **Next** shape (previewed so you can plan combos) slides in. It's endless — you're chasing a high score.

There's a move economy that keeps it tense: every move costs one move and every shape you clear earns several back, so you have to keep clearing or the run ends. There's no separate undo button — the move that *reverses* your last one is highlighted, and tapping it steps you back and refunds the move (you can keep undoing back to your last clear).

The modes layer on extra rules:

- **Shapes** — match the colour shape, any orientation.
- **Shapes + axis** — each candy also carries a *bar*, and the shape must come out with every bar lying the way the target shows (all upright or all sideways). A candy only turns 90° as it *leaves a corner* of its ring, so sliding along a straight edge keeps its facing; the centre candy spins with every ring move. There's a gentler *partial* variant where only some cells of the shape are bar-constrained and the rest take any facing.
- **Two shapes** — two different-colour shapes you have to form on the board *at the same time* to clear together.

The piece I'm happiest with is invisible: every target is generated from the colours actually on your board, so it's always achievable — no asking for three greens when there are only two. (Getting that right, and proving it, took a few rewrites.)
